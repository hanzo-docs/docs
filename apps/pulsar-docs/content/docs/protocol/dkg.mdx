Pulsar distributes key generation via a Pedersen DKG over the M-LWE module
ring $R_q^k$, with the Feldman-style verifiable secret sharing replaced by
the module-shaped Pedersen commitment scheme. The construction inherits
its soundness argument from the Ring-LWE version that ships in
[`luxfi/corona`](https://github.com/luxfi/corona), ported to the module
shape with constant overhead per coefficient slot.

## Why Pedersen, not trusted-dealer

The academic 2-round threshold ML-DSA literature largely assumes a
**trusted dealer**: a single party samples the master secret and hands out
shares. That model is incompatible with open public chains.

Pulsar's Pedersen DKG removes the trusted dealer: each party samples a
random polynomial over $R_q^k$, broadcasts a hiding commitment to it, and
emits shares of its polynomial to every other party. The aggregate group
secret is the sum of all parties' polynomials evaluated at zero; no single
party (and no proper subset of fewer than $t$ parties) ever holds the
group secret in clear.

## Round structure (sketch)

### Per-party setup

Party $i$ samples a degree-$(t-1)$ polynomial
$f_i: R_q^k \to R_q^k$ (one polynomial per module slot) with
$f_i(0) = \mathbf{a}_i \cdot \chi$ where $\chi$ is the M-LWE error
distribution and $\mathbf{a}_i$ is sampled uniformly. Party $i$ commits
to $f_i$ via the module-shaped Pedersen commitment scheme and broadcasts
the commitment.

### Share delivery

Party $i$ emits to each other party $j$ the share $f_i(j)$ via an
encrypted, authenticated point-to-point channel. The encryption uses an
ML-KEM-768 wrapper bound to the recipient's long-term identity key
(CR-6 closure, see [postmortem references in BLOCKERS.md](https://github.com/luxfi/pulsar/blob/main/BLOCKERS.md)).

### Verification

Each party $j$ verifies that every received share is consistent with the
broadcasted commitment. Inconsistent shares trigger an identifiable abort
with signed evidence.

### Aggregation

The group secret share at party $j$ is $s_j = \sum_i f_i(j)$. The group
public key is

$$
\mathbf{t} = \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{s_1} + \mathbf{s_2}
$$

where $\mathbf{s_1}, \mathbf{s_2}$ are the Lagrange-reconstructed group
secrets at quorum-evaluation points 0.

## Class N4 — public-key preservation across resharing

Proactive resharing rotates the share custodians without changing the
group public key. For long-lived deployments (multi-epoch validator set),
the relevant adversary model is mobile (HJKY97): the adversary may corrupt
up to $t-1$ parties **per epoch**, with the corruption set varying.

Pulsar's reshare:

1. The outgoing quorum samples fresh randomness and emits re-shared
   contributions for the incoming quorum.
2. The incoming quorum aggregates the contributions to form fresh shares
   of the same group secret.
3. Public-key continuity is verified: the new shares Lagrange-reconstruct
   to the same $\mathbf{s_1}$ as the previous epoch.

The Class N4 theorem `reshare_preserves_secret_honest` in
`proofs/easycrypt/Pulsar_N4.ec` proves public-key continuity over the
abstract reshare module under the honest-execution model. The Lean-bridged
algebraic axioms `add_share_zeroR`, `reconstruct_linear`, and
`shamir_correct` discharge the additive structure on shares; see
[Lean ↔ EC bridge](/docs/proofs/lean-bridge).

## Beacon-randomized quorum selection

For the proactive resharing path specifically, the **quorum selection** must
not be deterministic. A mobile adversary that knows the deterministic
quorum-selection rule can pre-position corruptions to land in the
selected quorum every epoch, defeating the mobile-adversary security
argument.

Pulsar's reshare selects the per-epoch quorum via a beacon-derived
randomness commit at the epoch boundary (HIP-0077 §"reshare quorum" F10
fix). Production deployments bind this to the consensus layer's existing
randomness beacon.

## Identifiable abort

If any party deviates during DKG or reshare — emits an inconsistent
share, omits a round message, broadcasts an invalid commitment — the
remaining honest parties identify the deviating party and produce signed
evidence suitable for slashing.

Pulsar identifies deviations under synchronous network assumptions only;
asynchronous identifiable abort is out of scope for this submission.

## Out-of-scope adversary capabilities

- Compromise of the cryptographic hash function (SHAKE-256 / cSHAKE-256 /
  KMAC-256). NIST FIPS 202 + SP 800-185 are taken as building blocks.
- Side-channel attacks on the physical hardware running a party (power
  analysis, EM, fault injection). Handled at the deployed-binary layer;
  the algorithm-correctness chain is independent.
- Compromise of the BIP-32/BIP-39 mnemonic from which device-specific
  keys are derived.
